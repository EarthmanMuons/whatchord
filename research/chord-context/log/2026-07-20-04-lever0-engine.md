# 2026-07-20: Lever 0 engine rule shipped

**Goal.** Implement the decided m7/6 rule in the engine (entries 2026-07-20-01
through -03), verify parity with the offline simulation, and clear the remaining
adoption gates.

**Setup.** Engine change: a new tonality-gated tie-breaker,
`prefer key-functional seventh over sixth-chord twin`
(`packages/whatchord/lib/src/analysis/rules/six_chord_rules.dart`), positioned
after the voicing-evidence rule and before the four tonality-blind rules that
previously decided this family. It prefers the relative seventh reading over its
sixth-chord twin exactly in the decided cells (minor7 on degree ii either mode;
halfDiminished7 on degree ii in minor or on the leading tone in either mode),
whatever the bass. Unit tests pin the fire and no-fire cases including the
decided borrowed major-key iiø65 (stays m6) and tonic sixths
(`test/key_functional_seventh_rule_test.dart`); the pre-existing common-name tie
test was transposed to degree iii to preserve its fallback-coverage intent, with
a companion pinning the new rule on the original pair. The chord-debug
explanation map gained the rule's entry. `lever0_eval.dart` gained an `engine`
arm: a full re-rank under the inferred-key context (the production path), with a
parity count against the simulated flip.

```sh
mise run research:chord-context-lever0-dcml-dev
mise run research:chord-context-lever0-wir-dev
python3 tool/chord-context/lever0_compare.py build/chord-context/lever0/dcml-dev/report.json --arm engine
python3 tool/chord-context/lever0_compare.py build/chord-context/lever0/wir-dev/report.json --arm engine
tool/benchmark.sh --check
```

**What happened.**

- Parity: the engine path disagrees with the simulated flip on 152 of 54,570
  DCML events (0.28%) and 2 of 1,594 When-in-Rome events. This is the general
  tonality-context churn of re-ranking under a sometimes-wrong inferred key (the
  old closed-loop probe measured ~0.4%), not the rule misfiring; on When-in-Rome
  the engine and simulation accuracies are identical (98.93%).
- Accuracy through the production path (engine arm vs. base, paired): DCML
  97.80% -> 98.46%, mean per-piece delta +0.0062, CI95 [+0.0036, +0.0091], 170
  wins / 52 losses / 701 ties, p = 6.3e-14; When-in-Rome 97.87% -> 98.93%,
  +0.0051, CI95 [+0.0010, +0.0106], 6/0/50, p = 3.6e-02. The engine arm's losing
  pieces are the churn events above (full tonality context moves other near-ties
  too, both directions, netting strongly positive); the rule-only simulation
  remains zero-loss. Note base itself rose (97.54% -> 97.80% on DCML) because
  the fixtures' neutral C-major context now legitimately applies the rule where
  its degrees happen to match.
- Gates: full package and app suites pass (including the transposed tie test);
  `tool/benchmark.sh --check` PASS with every deterministic counter unchanged
  (the rule adds comparator work only); the no-fire behavior is pinned by unit
  tests, which is the invariance guard's form under the always-deliberate-key
  wiring decision.
- CHANGELOG updated (user-facing naming change).

**Plain-English reading.** The engine now does what the offline experiment
promised: in a key context, the D-F-A-C-over-F family reads as the seventh chord
musicians expect mid-progression, tonic sixths keep their names, and the handful
of differences between the shipped path and the idealized simulation are the key
detector occasionally being wrong about the key, which costs a little on some
pieces and pays more on others.

**Decisions.**

- Adopted. The rule ships as an always-on tonality-gated tie-breaker per the
  wiring decision in entry -03.
- The ranking-algorithm article
  (`docs/site/articles/chord-recognition-algorithm.html`) is updated in the same
  change: the new rule appears in the ordered tie-breaker list, and the C-E-G-A
  example now notes the key-conditional outcome.
